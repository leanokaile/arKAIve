SO BE IT.

My faith is ever inconsistent, similar to how I tend to uphold my routines. Somehow, at the end of the day, things get done and everything falls into place just as it should be. I am agnostic. I am open to the existence of God or the higher being, but I do not devote my time as any other Catholics would. Praying to the unknowable is sounding criminally immoral. It’s a sick thing to admit that I believe in the higher being just as I believe that there are plans set before our grave stones. Lately, my belief has been challenged and proven right — that everything does happen for a reason. In a series of unfortunate events and in order to not succumb to the terrifying events of life, I desperately held on to the thought that: if this is happening to me now, there must be a reason for it; God must have a plan, right? If this won’t go well, I’ll take it as a sign that it’s not meant for me. Directly speaking, portraying Oedipus, I’d commit to the decision of not knowing my own fate and go on with life as if I am doing it in my own free will. This kind of belief entitles me to the feeling that I deserve whatever it is that is coming toward my direction. There is a reason for everything; therefore, I accept what is happening now and shall not dwell on it tomorrow. There’s a satisfaction knowing that how I react is a matter of my own free will despite many arguments saying that it’s nonexistent. Oedipus blinding himself may as well be a testament to this ‘nonexistent free will’.

As humans, we cling to the social constructs of the people in order to explain what cannot be explained. To keep our sanity intact, we label the terms unbeknownst to us most likely because we are afraid of unlabeled terms. Some may argue that the belief in God is extremely superficial and created by the human society to embrace the unexplainable origin of life and how it evolved to what it is now, and some may also argue that it is a factual history rooted from passing down stories from generations to another resulting to different perspectives having a common denominator and finally colliding into one. I used to ask myself where I sat in between all of this only to find out that the world won’t end if I just sat idly in the middle. If we believe in the divine will, we may not be able to overcome it, because if we seem to be able to surpass what is written for us, then it might just be the divine will’s order that we are fated to surpass it. An endless limbo, if I must say. However, I do believe that surrendering ourselves to the higher plan will not earn us the title of ‘The most rational species to ever exist’. But that also doesn’t mean that we should find a way to counter it. Given the example that Oedipus knew he was bound to harm and end his father’s life and marry his mother — does not justify the ends to his decision. Finding a way to counter his fate was also the very reason the fate was done. At this point, I have slightly driven myself mad just by thinking of a loophole. Let’s say that I am fated to become a Doctor. Knowing that fate would make me feel complacent if I were to commit myself to believing that it will happen. On the other hand, if I wanted to become an engineer and despise the fate of becoming a doctor, I will then continue my life just as I want to direct it. If in the end, I do become a Doctor, then that means that the divine will is real and I have no escape; but if I became an Engineer at the end, believers in fate may argue that I was destined to change my predetermined profession.

So why overthink it in the first place if it’ll just go back to that endless loop of mental prison? If fate is as strong as it seems in Oedipus Rex, then I'll gladly raise my middle finger for it and not dwell on the will-be’s of life. Humans can either live a purpose-driven life or a life without meaning at all. It is their decision. And if I want to use the logic that “everything happens for a reason” to describe the unexplainable events in life, then so be it. We won’t have unlimited breaths to waste on the wonders of whether we are living our lives for us or we’re just living it because it is how we are meant to live. More often than not, it really means a lot not to care about everything just for a moment.
