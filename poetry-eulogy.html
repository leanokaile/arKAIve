A Eulogy, maybe.

Poetry • arKAIve Collection
I pour my heart out on my poetry like how my grandfather used to pour liquor in his thick, dark, mug bottoms-up like it's a cup of water. How I wonder if that's the reason why the left side of his body is frozen in time as the other half still beats lively. His stories, like aged whiskey, swirl in my mind, intoxicating, each word a sip of fact or tale, each pause a breath of loathing ang longing. How can a poison taste so sweet and confusing? The wrinkles on his face map battles we fought and won. He watched by the bench on my swim meet in which I garnered medals I don't even own. I dive in a pool of embarrassment, while his stance pose a testament that I am his blood as his pride will not show any dents. Unpleasant or not, the images in my young mind has not ceased to rot. The scenes of distastefulness is what might've shaped what I got as I grew up in a perfectionist’s melting pot. One calm morning came with a ray of daily routine when God says “I think it's time to bury half of what is seen.” He was rushed through the aisles of my current dream, while my mother cries after I picked up my phone on the first sharp ring. Although he breathes normally, there's the fear growing inside. There's no way of knowing how to stop grieving for those that are alive. For now, I will carry half of what is left. My smiles won't falter as it is how I am always dressed.
