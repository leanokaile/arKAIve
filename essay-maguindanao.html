Hoarse and defiant voices: forever undeterred

Feature Article • 2026 • arKAIve Collection

Once a vibrant landscape of blue and green, Barangay Salman is now steeped in a somber soil, compelled to house restless souls of both the dead and the living. Fifteen years have passed, yet the ground remains a perilous reminder of the tragedy. Despite the passage of time, the pursuit of justice and the fight against impunity in the Philippines remain unwavering.

How many journalists must be silenced before their yearning voices penetrate through the country’s skin and bones? One thing remains certain: the relentless pursuit of truth and the memory of that fateful day will never cease. The 23rd day of November, 2009 is engraved in a medium that will never fade.

Smelting ores to chains: a robust tool abetting the political dynasty

In the mid-1970s, the Ampatuan clan emerged as the most influential family in Maguindanao, their power bolstered by the support of then-President Ferdinand Marcos Sr. However, it was in 2001 that their dominance truly soared. With the election of Gloria Macapagal-Arroyo as President, an executive order was issued, legalizing the formation of private armies controlled by high-status families like the Ampatuans.

The clan's grip extended over the police, judiciary, and local election commission. In essence, the Ampatuans' word became law. Opposing them was akin to digging one's own grave, as starkly stated by Suwaib Upahm, an Ampatuan militia member, on March 9, 2010 – who is also a prosecution witness killed by unidentified gunmen in Parang, Maguindanao after claiming to have information regarding the massacre.

Emerging rust: attempt to breaking the robust chain

In the lead-up to the May 2010 elections, Esmael “Toto” Mangudadatu had garnered significant support from the people of Maguindanao, which unfortunately led to death threats against him. On November 23, 2009, his pregnant wife and several female relatives set out for the provincial capital to file his candidacy on his behalf, accompanied by media personnel to document the event. Mangudadatu, believing that the threats would not extend to women, felt a false sense of security. Tragically, this assumption proved fatally wrong, highlighting that violence knows no gender boundaries.

The convoy, which included Mangudadatu’s relatives, six passersby on a motorcycle, and over 30 journalists, was ambushed and forced off the highway. They were brutally executed with high-powered firearms. In a grim attempt to cover up the massacre, three shallow mass graves were dug in the hills of Sitio Masalay. Only 57 bodies were recovered, as the body of photojournalist Reynaldo “Bebot” Momay has never been found.

Chains on stronghold: judiciary and politicians on both ends

The Ampatuan clan's grip on the press was suffocating, stifling freedom and silencing voices. This tragedy, known as the Maguindanao massacre, drew national and international attention, igniting protests and calls for justice for the journalists who were brutally murdered. Under immense public pressure, the administration of Gloria Macapagal-Arroyo arrested Andal Ampatuan Sr., his son Andal Ampatuan Jr., the main suspect who was also planning to run for governor, and Zaldy Ampatuan, the brother of the main suspect. All pleaded not guilty in court.

Despite their denials, the evidence against the Ampatuans was overwhelming. The trial became a symbol of the fight against impunity in the Philippines, highlighting the need for judicial reform and the protection of press freedom. The massacre not only exposed the deep-rooted corruption within the political system but also galvanized a movement for change. As the world watched, the relentless pursuit of justice for the victims continued, serving as a stark reminder of the power of the press and the importance of holding those in power accountable.

Rusty and functioning chains against unyielding calls for justice

Esmael "Toto" Mangudadatu continued his political career with unwavering determination, advocating for justice for the victims of the Ampatuan massacre and exposing the family's documented corruption. He served as the Governor of Maguindanao from 2010 to 2019, and later as the congressman for Maguindanao's 2nd district. Despite facing numerous death threats, Mangudadatu remained steadfast in his mission.

Meanwhile, the Ampatuan clan's political influence persisted in other areas of the Philippines. Many family members continued to hold elective or appointed positions in the province's municipalities, maintaining their grip on power even as some members served prison sentences for their roles in the massacre.

As of 2024, 44 suspects have been convicted, while 88 remain at large. The families of the victims continue to seek justice and compensation, expressing frustration over the slow progress and feeling forgotten by those who once supported them. The 15th anniversary of the massacre saw journalists and families gathering at the site to remember the victims and call for long-overdue justice.
